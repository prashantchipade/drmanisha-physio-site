Shoulder Pain

Why Does My Shoulder Hurt, and Could It Be Serious? Shoulder pain is a common symptom with many potential causes, including rotator cuff injuries, arthritis, bursitis, dislocations, and referred pain from the neck. Treatment for minor pain may include rest, ice or heat, over-the-counter pain relievers, and gentle movement. See a doctor for severe pain, persistent symptoms after few days, sudden pain, inability to move the arm, or signs of a dislocated or fractured bone.

Common Causes

Rotator Cuff Problems: Inflammation or tears in the tendons of the rotator cuff, the group of muscles that provide wide range of motion.
Arthritis: Degeneration of the cartilage in the shoulder joint.
Bursitis: Inflammation of the fluid-filled sacs that cushion the joint, often occurring with rotator cuff issues.
Frozen Shoulder: Stiffness in the shoulder joint that makes movement difficult and painful.
Shoulder Dislocation: The shoulder joint coming out of its socket.
Fractures: A broken bone in the shoulder or collarbone.
Impingement Syndrome: Tendons and bursae becoming trapped in the space under the "roof" of the shoulder, causing inflammation and pain.
Referred Pain: Pain that originates from another area, such as the neck or a spinal nerve problem, and is felt in the shoulder.

When to See a Doctor

You should seek medical advice for shoulder pain if you experience:

Sudden, severe pain.
Inability to move your arm.
A change in shape, significant swelling, or numbness in the arm or shoulder.
Pins and needles or burning sensations.
Pain that doesn't improve after two weeks of self-care.

Home Care for Minor Shoulder Pain

Rest: Avoid activities that worsen the pain.
Ice: Apply ice packs for 15-20 minutes every few hours to reduce pain and swelling.
Gentle Movement: Stay active and gently move your shoulder to prevent it from becoming stiffer.

How to avoid Shoulder Pain

Improve Posture: Sit and stand with your shoulders back, chest open, and avoid slouching, especially if you have a desk job.
Adjust Your Workspace: Set up your computer and workstation to support an ergonomic and healthy posture.
Take Breaks: Move around and take breaks during repetitive tasks or long periods of sitting to reduce strain on your shoulders.
Avoid Overloading: Don't carry heavy items that strain your shoulders, and try to distribute weight evenly rather than carrying bags on one shoulder.
